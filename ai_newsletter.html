Healthcare & Pharma

AI Is Rewriting the Rules of Drug Discovery — and the Numbers Are Hard to Ignore

Novo Nordisk's partnership with OpenAI and Amazon's new research tool signal a fundamental shift in how pharmaceutical companies find their next blockbuster drug.

Drug discovery has historically been one of the most expensive and time-consuming processes in any industry. A single approved medication requires an average of 10 to 20 years of development and costs approximately $2.6 billion. Most candidates fail. The economics have been brutal.

In April 2026 that model is being dismantled in real time. Novo Nordisk announced a partnership with OpenAI to integrate AI across its entire business — from drug discovery and clinical trials through to manufacturing and supply chains. The stated goal is to accelerate identification of new obesity and diabetes treatments. Amazon simultaneously launched a dedicated AI research tool specifically for early-stage drug discovery.

"AI-powered drug discovery programs are identifying promising compounds in two weeks — work that previously took research teams six months."

The commercial case is clear. McKinsey estimates generative AI alone could save the pharmaceutical industry $60 to $110 billion annually. Early adopters report 25% faster discovery timelines and up to 70% reductions in clinical trial costs. The global AI in drug discovery market, valued at $24.5 billion in 2026, is forecast to reach $160 billion by 2035.

The regulatory environment is also catching up. The FDA's draft AI guidance is expected to be finalized in 2026, and the EU AI Act's high-risk provisions take effect in August, requiring detailed documentation on model architectures and training data for pharmaceutical AI applications. The companies that have built auditable, explainable AI pipelines early will have a significant compliance advantage.

Finance & Supply Chain

Oracle's Agentic Platform Puts AI Directly Inside Corporate Banking and Supply Chain Workflows

Twelve new AI agents embedded in Oracle Fusion Cloud are taking over the administrative burden that has slowed enterprise finance and supply chain teams for decades.

On April 9, 2026, Oracle introduced Fusion Agentic Applications for finance and supply chain — a suite of twelve AI-driven applications embedded directly into Oracle Fusion Cloud. The agents do not sit alongside existing workflows. They operate inside them, with access to enterprise data, approval hierarchies, and transactional context.

The business problem being addressed is real and well understood by anyone who has worked in enterprise finance. Corporate banking, treasury, and supply chain operations involve enormous volumes of compliance-heavy manual work — extracting information from loan documents, monitoring risk thresholds, drafting review narratives, reconciling discrepancies between systems. These are tasks that consume analyst hours without requiring the judgment that senior analysts are actually paid to apply.

"Finance and supply chain teams are under constant pressure to close faster, respond to disruptions sooner, and deliver more with the same resources — but this is extremely difficult when so much time is tied up in manual follow-ups and handoffs."

The Oracle platform handles the administrative layer autonomously, surfacing exceptions and edge cases for human judgment rather than routine processing. The company reports that early deployments are reducing manual workflow time by approximately 50% in targeted functions. For supply chain teams specifically, the agents monitor for disruption signals and recommend alternative sourcing paths in real time — a capability that has obvious value for any organization navigating today's geopolitically sensitive component markets.

The framework includes observability and ROI measurement tools, which addresses one of the most common blockers to enterprise AI adoption: the inability to demonstrate concrete returns to leadership. Oracle has designed accountability into the deployment rather than leaving it as an afterthought.

Semiconductors

As the Semiconductor Industry Approaches $1 Trillion, AI Is Running the Supply Chain Behind It

Demand forecasting, yield optimization, and risk management in the semiconductor supply chain are increasingly driven by AI — with direct implications for how companies like Qualcomm plan and source.

Global semiconductor revenues are forecast to exceed $975 billion in 2026 — approaching a historic $1 trillion threshold — driven almost entirely by AI infrastructure demand. Memory and logic ICs are growing at over 30% year-on-year. The scale is unprecedented, and managing the supply chain that supports it has become a fundamentally different problem than it was five years ago.

AI is now embedded at every layer of semiconductor supply chain management. TSMC has implemented AI for demand prediction and inventory optimization, achieving measurable cost savings and improved production timelines. Samsung uses AI to monitor supply chain state and anticipate disruptions caused by material scarcity or transportation issues. Qualcomm has deployed AI models specifically to assess supplier understanding and manage geopolitical risk — generating sourcing plan recommendations based on real-time signals.

"AI is operationalized at Qualcomm to assess the understanding of suppliers and guarantee sound risk management frameworks, analyzing geopolitical risks and providing recommendations on alternative sourcing."

The yield management application is particularly significant. A single microscopic flaw in chip fabrication renders an entire chip unusable — a direct hit to profitability at volume. AI-driven yield management analyzes vast manufacturing datasets to predict potential defects before they occur, improving throughput and reducing waste. Intel has deployed automatic defect search systems, replacing much of what was previously manual inspection.

The advanced packaging bottleneck — techniques like 3D stacking and CoWoS — has become the primary limiting factor for AI chip supply in 2026. AI-driven scheduling and materials planning tools are being applied here to reduce lead times and optimize capacity allocation across constrained packaging nodes. Companies that can model packaging capacity accurately have a material competitive advantage in securing AI chip supply.

The Bottom Line

The three industries examined in this edition — pharmaceuticals, enterprise finance, and semiconductors — represent very different business contexts, but the pattern is consistent. AI is not being deployed to replace human judgment. It is being deployed to eliminate the administrative and computational burden that sits between humans and the judgment calls they are actually paid to make.

In drug discovery, AI compresses the search space so researchers spend less time running experiments that fail. In corporate banking, AI handles document extraction and routine monitoring so analysts can focus on exceptions and client relationships. In semiconductor supply chains, AI models geopolitical risk and demand signals so planners can make faster, more informed sourcing decisions.

The common thread is augmentation — and the organizations moving fastest are the ones that identified specific, high-volume manual workflows and asked what it would take to automate the routine while keeping humans in the loop for judgment. That framing is more durable than chasing the latest model release. It is also more honest about what AI is actually good at right now.
